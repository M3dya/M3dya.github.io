Finch 3D

Making the first step obvious in a generative design platform that asks a lot of every new user.

The Problem

A powerful tool that overwhelms on day one

Finch 3D is a generative design platform for early-stage architecture. It takes a building massing imported from tools like Rhino, Revit, or Grasshopper and automatically populates it with floor plans—testing unit mixes, checking code compliance, and returning performance data in real time.

That power is also the problem. A new user arrives and meets the entire system at once: an AI core, three connected capability areas, and external tools feeding in from every side—with no guidance on where to begin. My job was to make the first step obvious.

The System

Everything a new user faces at once

Before designing the onboarding, I mapped the full ecosystem to understand exactly how much sits in front of a first-time user. The AI core is surrounded by its own outputs and ringed by the external tools that feed it.

The Finch 3D ecosystem: AI core, its capability outputs, and the external tools feeding it.

How It Works

Not pretty pictures — a constraint engine

Understanding the core mattered before I could simplify the entry point. The engine isn’t generating images; it’s solving a constrained optimization problem on a graph. The user defines a footprint, the rooms and areas they need, and constraints for adjacency, daylight, structural grids, and circulation—then the engine generates many compliant layouts at once, each scored on real metrics.

It does not replace design thinking. It compresses the exploration phase—turning days of manual iteration into minutes—so architects can evaluate more options before committing.

The critical insight for onboarding: the graph rules are where new users get stuck. Skip them, and you spend more time rejecting unusable layouts than the tool saves. The first step a user takes isn’t generating—it’s defining the rules well. That reframing became the spine of the onboarding work.

Outcome

A clearer way in

By treating rule-definition as the true first step rather than an afterthought, the onboarding guides users toward the inputs that determine whether the generated output is usable—reducing the early frustration of filtering bad results and getting people to a confident first layout faster.
