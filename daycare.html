Ontario Daycare Access

Affordability got solved. Access broke.

Service DesignUX ResearchCivicPro Bono
Role
UX Researcher & Service Designer
Duration
8 weeks
Focus
Ontario childcare access
The human stakes

Meet Uma

Uma is 33, an immigrant parent in Toronto. This year she went back to work with no childcare secured—driven by financial pressure, not choice. She applied to more than twenty centers and got back only generic email acknowledgements. After a year and a half on waitlists she still has no position, no timeline, and no spot. Her family patches care together day to day with informal help.

“Even after applying 1.5 years back, still nothing. Still waiting.”

— Uma, parent (R1)

The easy read is that Uma was unlucky, or started too late. She wasn’t. She is the system working exactly as designed. Once I started talking to other parents, her story stopped looking like an exception and started looking like the rule.

What actually broke

The program that helped—and made it worse

Ontario’s $10-a-day childcare program (CWELCC) was meant to help, and in one way it did: it fixed affordability. But it poured a wave of new demand into a system that was never built to handle it—and it created zero new spots.

So more parents now compete for the same spaces, using the same fragmented, manual process as before. The government solved the cost of daycare and, without meaning to, made finding it harder. Affordability got solved; access got worse.

The villain

A black box with no tracking number

The deeper I went, the clearer the real problem became. It isn’t cost anymore—it’s not knowing. Parents can’t see their position on a waitlist, can’t see a timeline, and don’t know the rules by which spots are handed out. They apply, and then they wait in silence.

“A lot of anxiety wondering when a spot would open up, or when someone would get back to me.”

— R1

I mapped the full end-to-end service to find where the experience breaks. It breaks in one place, hard: the waitlist. That’s where the wait stretches from months to over a year, where providers go quiet, and where parent stress peaks.

Current-state service blueprint. The waitlist is the critical failure point—stress hits 5/5 exactly where communication goes silent.

Proof it’s systemic

Not bad luck—a broken system

If this were just Uma, it would be a sad story. It isn’t. Across the research, one pain point came up for every single parent: not knowing where they stood. Transparency wasn’t the top complaint—it was the universal one.

Pain points by how often parents raised them. Waitlist transparency was the only one mentioned by everyone.

The most telling detail: even parents who had a smooth experience and already secured a spot still asked for more transparency. When people who “won” the process still want it changed, the problem isn’t the individual—it’s the design.

“Real-time updates on availability and waitlists, so parents don’t have to follow up constantly.”

— R2

And the workaround everyone invented was the same: a personal spreadsheet. Parents were hand-building the tracking system the service should have given them.

“Maybe they can all use the same waitlist website.”

— R1
Why nothing fixes it

Three layers of tools, one missing bridge

If parents are in pain and providers are drowning, why hasn’t a tool solved this? I ran a competitive analysis across everything in the market. The answer: every tool covers part of the journey, and none of them bridge it.

Discovery tools, provider tools, and government portals—none bridge discovery to enrollment. That gap is the whole problem.

There’s one exception in Canada. Quebec launched a centralized, government-mandated portal where every licensed provider must participate, parents apply once, and a monthly rank indicator shows roughly where they stand. It’s the only system that closes the gap—and it took a mandate to do it. Ontario has no equivalent.

Quebec proved a centralized model works—and showed three places a next version could go further.

The vision

It should feel like package tracking

Here’s the shift in one sentence. When you order a package, you get a tracking number: you know where it is, roughly when it arrives, and you’re told if something changes. Right now, parents have sent a package with no tracking number and no estimated delivery date.

The research points to a service that works for both sides at once—because any tool that fixes only one side has already failed. It rests on a few principles that came straight out of what parents told me:

  • Transparency over completeness. Parents need to know where they stand more than they need a perfect system. Even a rough position reduces anxiety.
  • One action, many applications. The single most requested change—apply once, not to twenty centers.
  • Proactive over reactive. Providers who reach out first transform the experience. Make that the default, not the exception.
  • Make the rules visible. When parents understand how spots are allocated, trust grows—even if the answer isn’t the one they wanted.
  • Mobile-first, low-friction. Parents search on their phones while holding a baby. Every extra step loses someone.
Intellectual honesty

What I still need to validate

A good case study shows its open edges. These are the questions I’d test before building anything:

Stress?
One parent said more information increased her anxiety. How much visibility is the right amount?
2 min
If updating the system takes providers longer than two minutes, they won’t do it. What’s the lightest possible input?
Who owns it?
Government, nonprofit, or private—this changes everything about trust and adoption, especially after Quebec’s 2021 data breach.

That’s the real finding under all of it: the problem was never that parents couldn’t afford daycare. It’s that the system was never designed for the demand affordability created—and the fix has to be built around the one thing parents kept asking for. To know where they stand.
