Translating 24 years of government data into a policy-ready equity narrative

The evidence existed — but it was buried across eleven dense Statistics Canada reports spanning 24 years, in language no policymaker has time to parse.

RoleLead UX Researcher & Information Designer
ClientGlocal Foundation of Canada
Year2024
The Problem

Authoritative data that couldn’t move anyone

Glocal needed to advocate to government on gender inequality in Canada’s labour market. The evidence existed — but it was buried across eleven dense Statistics Canada reports spanning 24 years, in language no policymaker has time to parse.

This wasn’t a data-availability problem. The numbers were authoritative and public. It was a communication problem: scattered, statistical, and without a throughline, the evidence couldn’t travel into the rooms where decisions get made.

Advocacy to government is only as strong as its evidence — and only as effective as that evidence is legible. No single source told the whole story, and decision-makers act on clear, defensible narratives, not on tables.

My role

I led the work end to end: sourced and synthesized two decades of federal data, found the narrative that connected it, and designed how that narrative would reach a government audience — a twelve-panel evidence brief built to be absorbed, trusted, and acted on.

Why the evidence didn’t land

Three barriers, compounding

Fragmentation

Eleven separate reports, each with its own scope and timeframe. The full picture existed only if someone assembled it.

Inaccessibility

Wage gaps, percentage-point shifts, R&D differentials, automation risk — accurate, but not absorbable under time pressure.

No throughline

Each figure was undeniable alone; together they had no “so what.” The argument was invisible until constructed.

Research Process → Solution Design

From eleven reports to one argument

Synthesize the evidence

Worked through all eleven StatCan reports, extracting the load-bearing findings and reconciling them across inconsistent timeframes and definitions to find a single defensible thesis.

Name the thesis

It emerged clearly: equality of access has improved; equality of outcome has not. That tension became the spine of the entire brief.

Architect the narrative

Sequenced twelve panels as a deliberate argument — context, gains, persistent barriers, intersectional reality, then implication and call to action — so the order itself does the persuading.

Design for comprehension

Built a scannable visual system: one idea per panel, consistent encoding (paired bars for the narrowing wage gap, dials for representation), and an institutional palette that reads as credible, not promotional.

The Solution

The twelve-panel evidence brief

The finished artifact carries the full argument in twelve sequenced panels — progress and barrier held in deliberate tension from the opening frame to the call to act.

The complete brief — synthesized from eleven Statistics Canada reports, 2000–2024

Key Decisions

Three choices, each a real tradeoff

Pair every gain with a barrier

Too positive and the advocacy collapses; too negative and the audience disengages. Pairing each progress panel with a barrier kept it honest and made the thesis unavoidable.

Give intersectionality its own space

The easy cut was a footnote. Instead, Indigenous, racialized, immigrant, and disabled women got dedicated panels — aggregated data hides exactly what government needs to see.

Encode for comprehension

Not every statistic made the cut. One idea per panel, in the simplest form that preserves accuracy — attention, not data, was the binding constraint.

Reflection

What I’d do next

The brief rests on a strong analytical thesis and audience-aware design — but its persuasive effect on policymakers is currently a designed hypothesis, not a tested one. The natural next iteration is to validate it against the people it’s meant to move.

Intellectual honesty

I’d run a short comprehension study with four to five stakeholders in the target audience, focused on the densest panels (R&D investment, automation risk), to confirm the takeaways land in seconds rather than minutes. I’d also test the progress-first order against a barrier-first variant to measure which framing strengthens the call to action without triggering disengagement.
