Election Dashboard Feedback

Federal election data lived in dense spreadsheets and static reports — useful to researchers, invisible to everyone else. I helped shape an interactive dashboard that lets anyone filter Canadian voter turnout by year, province, age group, and gender, surfacing a pattern buried in the raw numbers: youth turnout consistently trails the national average.

Problem Inside the dashboard Outcome
Problem

Data that only researchers could read

Canada's federal election turnout figures — broken down by province, age group, gender, and year — already existed in public datasets. But the data lived exactly where most people would never look: multi-tab spreadsheets and static PDF reports.

Somewhere in the numbers was a real, repeating story — youth turnout lagging behind the national average, election after election. But a spreadsheet doesn't tell a story. It just sits there until someone with the right tools goes looking.

The goal wasn't to collect new data — it was to make the data that already existed legible: build something a volunteer, a policymaker, or a curious citizen could open, filter, and immediately understand.

Inside the Dashboard

Filter by year, province, age, and gender

The dashboard's core interaction is simple on purpose: four filters let a user narrow the entire dataset down to exactly the slice they care about, with every chart on the page updating together.

Layered together, the filters and charts make the pattern impossible to miss: across nearly every election from 2004 to 2021, youth turnout sits below the turnout of the broader selected age group — visible in seconds, instead of buried in a cross-tab.

Outcome

A dataset anyone can question

The dashboard turns a static, researcher-only dataset into something explorable. Instead of asking a research team to run a new cross-tab for every question, a user can now filter the data themselves — by province, by year, by age group, by gender — and see the answer rendered as a chart in real time.
