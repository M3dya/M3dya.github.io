Case Study · Glocal Foundation · 01 of 3

Fourteen menu items, one confused volunteer

The live site scattered its three core volunteer programs across the navigation with no grouping and no clear starting point. An audit, a moderated usability session, and a validated proposal cut the top level from fourteen items to six.

RoleUX Researcher
MethodIA Audit & Survey
Sourceglocalfoundation.ca
Problem

A missed apply button, live on video

In a moderated usability session, a volunteer candidate was asked to find and apply to an online volunteering position. She read the whole page, scanned the paragraph text twice, and never noticed the button that would have taken her there — because it read as a plain hyperlink buried mid-paragraph, not a call to action.

“This link seems like it will take me there.”— usability session participant, describing a paragraph link she wasn’t sure was clickable

That single moment was symptomatic of the whole site. The navigation itself listed fourteen top-level items, with the three actual volunteer programs — Online Volunteering, Microgrants, and the Civic Essay Contest — each sitting on its own instead of grouped, competing for attention with “Get Involved,” which overlapped them in purpose but not in structure.

Research

What the audit and the session both surfaced

I ran a full IA audit of the live site’s navigation alongside a moderated usability session, then cross-referenced both against a short validation survey testing a proposed alternative structure.

Too many top-level choices

Fourteen items forced visitors to scan the entire bar before acting, instead of predicting where things lived.

Programs scattered, not grouped

Online Volunteering, Microgrants, the Essay Contest, and YouCount.ca each sat as separate, unrelated links.

Menu and footer disagreed

Reports & Publications and other resources appeared only in the footer, never in the navigation itself.

The usability session added the human texture behind those structural findings — participants weren’t just lost in the menu, they were lost in the copy and calls to action built on top of it:

“I would expect an easy CTA button instead of a link to lead me there.”— usability session participant
“This is a lot of text to take in.”— usability session participant, on the volunteering programs page

Domain jargon compounded the problem: the term “grassroots team” appeared repeatedly with no prior definition, and participants couldn’t connect it to the volunteering positions they were trying to find. One participant also flagged that a duplicate question later in the application — asking about academic background a second time — made the whole form feel less trustworthy, not more thorough.

Solution

Six labelled groups instead of fourteen loose items

The proposed structure grouped all three programs under one “Programs” menu, made “Get Involved” the single obvious starting point instead of a competing link, and folded Reports & Publications into a visible “Resources” menu so navigation and footer told the same story.

Before · 14 items

Programs spread across four separate links. “Get Involved” overlaps them. The footer adds Reports & Explore that aren’t in the menu at all.

After · 6 items

Three programs live under one menu. Everything a volunteer needs to start sits under “Get Involved.” Nothing hides only in the footer.

Validation

Testing the proposal, not just proposing it

Rather than ship the redesigned structure on instinct, I built it as a clickable prototype and ran it past reviewers through a short survey, asking them to complete the same tasks that had tripped people up in the original usability session — finding a volunteer program, understanding what “Get Involved” meant, and locating past reports.

What this validates, and what it doesn’t

The survey confirms the proposed grouping is easier to predict and navigate than the live structure. It doesn’t yet confirm downstream effects like application completion rate — that would need a second round of testing once the structure ships.
